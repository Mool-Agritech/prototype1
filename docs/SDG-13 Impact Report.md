## 1. Executive Summary: Harm Reduction vs. Active Resilience

Traditional disaster relief and crop insurance mechanisms operate under a reactive paradigm. They measure physical damage long after a seasonal failure occurs and disburse financial relief with substantial lag times. In the target pilot region of Vidarbha, Maharashtra, government crop insurance schemes (such as PMFBY) suffer from an administrative lag of 6 to 12 months due to reliance on manual Crop Cutting Experiments (CCEs). This delay forces smallholders and informal tenant farmers to rely on predatory credit markets, sell productive assets, or resort to distress migration.

Project Mool transitions climate risk management from delayed harm reduction to active resilience. By deploying a multi-sensor, 3-layer satellite nowcast engine—synthesizing ISRO AWIFS, Sentinel-1/2, CHIRPS rainfall data, and ERA5 Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD)—Mool executes automated parametric payouts in under 48 hours following a verified climate stress event.

This rapid liquidity buffer protects household financial stability, maintains farm-level operational autonomy, and preserves capital needed to re-sow or transition toward agroecological practices.

| :--- | :--- | :--- |
